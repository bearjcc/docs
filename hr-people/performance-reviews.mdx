Autex runs two performance review cycles each year — a mid-year check-in in July and a full annual review in January. Understanding how each cycle works helps you prepare thoughtfully, engage in honest conversations with your manager, and connect your day-to-day work to longer-term growth and compensation decisions.

## The two review cycles

<CardGroup cols={2}>
  <Card title="Mid-Year Review (July)" icon="sun">
    Developmental only. This cycle focuses entirely on growth and feedback — no compensation decisions are made. It's a chance to recalibrate on goals, surface concerns early, and align with your manager before the year closes.
  </Card>
  <Card title="Annual Review (January)" icon="snowflake">
    The full cycle. This review informs compensation adjustments, promotions, and development planning for the year ahead. It follows the same structure as mid-year but on an extended timeline.
  </Card>
</CardGroup>

## Review timeline

The annual cycle runs across December and January, with compensation outcomes communicated in February. The mid-year cycle follows the same structure compressed into two weeks in July.

| Phase | Annual Cycle | Mid-Year Cycle |
|---|---|---|
| Self-assessment opens | December | Early July |
| Peer feedback collection | January, week 1 | July, week 1 |
| Manager writes reviews | January, weeks 2–3 | July, week 2 |
| Review conversations | January, week 4 | July, week 2 |
| Comp adjustments communicated | February | N/A |

## Self-assessment

When the review window opens, you'll receive a prompt in the HR portal to complete your self-assessment. This is your opportunity to frame your contributions in your own words before your manager writes their review.

The self-assessment covers four areas:

- **Accomplishments** — What did you achieve this period? What impact did your work have?
- **Areas of growth** — Where have you improved compared to the last review period?
- **Development areas** — What skills or capabilities do you want to build going forward?
- **Anything else your manager should know** — Context that might not be visible from the outside: competing priorities, personal challenges that affected your work, or contributions that happened behind the scenes.

<Tip>
  Be specific. Vague statements like "I contributed to the team" are hard for your manager to act on. Name the projects, describe the outcomes, and quantify impact where you can.
</Tip>

## Peer feedback

After self-assessments are submitted, you'll be asked to nominate **3–5 peers** who can speak to your work. Your manager reviews nominations and may add or adjust names to ensure balanced coverage.

Nominated peers receive a short feedback form through the HR portal. Responses are **confidential** — your manager sees the feedback, but individual reviewers are never identified by name in your written review.

When you receive a peer feedback request, treat it seriously:

- Focus on specific behaviors and outcomes you observed directly.
- Acknowledge both strengths and areas for growth.
- Avoid vague praise or vague criticism — both are unhelpful.

<Note>
  Because peer feedback is confidential, you won't know exactly who said what. If you have questions about themes in your feedback, bring them to your review conversation.
</Note>

## The review conversation

Your manager will share your written review **before** your scheduled 1:1 so you have time to read and process it. Don't wait until the meeting to open it.

Come to the conversation prepared:

- **Read the review in advance.** Note anything that surprised you, anything you want to discuss further, or anything you disagree with.
- **Bring questions.** This is a two-way conversation, not a presentation.

The review conversation typically covers:

1. **What went well** — strengths your manager observed, recognition of specific contributions
2. **What to work on** — areas for development, patterns worth addressing
3. **Success criteria for the next period** — what "good" looks like going into the next cycle

## Ratings

Each review includes an overall performance rating on a 1–5 scale. Ratings are calibrated across teams before they are finalized, so your manager's initial assessment may shift slightly through that process.

| Rating | Label | What it means |
|---|---|---|
| 5 | Exceptional | Consistently exceeded expectations with outsized impact on the team or company |
| 4 | Strong | Met expectations and often exceeded them; reliable high performer |
| 3 | Meets Expectations | Solid, consistent contributor who delivers on commitments |
| 2 | Developing | Some gaps in performance or behavior; an improvement plan may follow |
| 1 | Not Meeting Expectations | Significant performance concerns that require immediate attention |

<Note>
  The majority of employees fall in the 3–4 range, and that is by design. A rating of 3 — Meets Expectations — is not a bad rating. It means you are doing your job well. Reserve 5 for truly exceptional, differentiated contributions.
</Note>
