Autex has team members across multiple time zones, which means not everyone is online at the same time — and that's intentional. Making distributed work feel natural requires shared defaults and a little extra deliberateness around communication. This page explains how we approach it.

## Async-first is the default

The default expectation at Autex is that people respond **when they are working**, not immediately. A Slack message is not an interruption that demands an instant reply. When you send someone a message, assume they'll see it and respond within their working day — not within the next few minutes.

This means:
- **Write messages that stand on their own.** Include context, relevant links, and a clear ask so the recipient can act without a back-and-forth to understand what you need.
- **Don't send "hey" and wait.** Combine your greeting and your question in one message.
- **Don't follow up immediately.** If you haven't heard back within a reasonable window, check that you sent to the right channel or person before pinging again.

If something is genuinely urgent and time-sensitive, **say so explicitly** — for example: *"Need this by EOD PT today"* or *"Blocking my PR — can you review before your EOD?"* Without that signal, people will treat your message as non-urgent.

<Note>
  "Urgent" means it cannot wait for the recipient's next working session. Use the word deliberately — if everything is urgent, nothing is.
</Note>

## Core overlap hours

Each team establishes a **core overlap window** — a block of time when all team members are expected to be available for synchronous meetings and conversations. Outside of that window, async is the default.

**Check with your manager** for your team's current overlap window when you start. Overlap windows vary by team depending on where members are located.

<Tip>
  Block your overlap window on your calendar as "Available" so teammates can schedule meetings with you during that time without guessing.
</Tip>

## Scheduling meetings

Good meeting scheduling is a shared responsibility. Follow these practices to make it easier for everyone:

- **Share your working hours.** Add them to your Google Calendar and keep your Slack status up to date.
- **Use a scheduling tool** (such as Calendly) or offer multiple time options when reaching out to someone in a different time zone.
- **Rotate recurring meeting times** if a single time slot is consistently inconvenient for some attendees. No one person should always be the one taking a meeting at an off hour.
- **Record video meetings** whenever possible so people who couldn't attend can catch up asynchronously.

<Warning>
  Avoid scheduling meetings outside someone's listed working hours without asking first. Even if the calendar shows them "available," that doesn't mean outside-hours meetings are welcome.
</Warning>

## Communication response expectations

These are the response time expectations for each channel. Do not set faster expectations for non-urgent messages — faster expectations create pressure to be always-on, which undermines the async-first culture we're building.

| Channel | Expected response |
|---|---|
| Slack DM | Within your working day |
| Slack `@channel` / `@here` | Within your working day |
| Email | Within 24 hours on a working day |
| `@urgent` tag | As soon as reasonably possible |

<Note>
  The `@urgent` tag in Slack is for situations that genuinely can't wait. Use it sparingly — if it's overused, people stop treating it as a signal.
</Note>

## Out-of-office and availability

Keeping your status up to date is a small habit that makes a big difference in a distributed team. Others rely on your status to know whether to expect a response soon or to find someone else who can help.

- **Update your Slack status** when you're heads-down on focused work, out sick, or on vacation. Even a quick emoji and a note ("🏖️ OOO through Friday") goes a long way.
- **Set a calendar OOO event** for any absence longer than a day. Include an auto-reply if you won't be checking messages.
- **Set expectations before you go.** If you're taking time off, let your team know in advance and hand off anything time-sensitive before you leave.

## Working hours flexibility

There is no required 9-to-5 at Autex. You are responsible for:

1. **Meeting your commitments** — deadlines, deliverables, and anything you've told teammates to expect from you.
2. **Being available during your team's core overlap window** for synchronous collaboration.

Outside of those two things, how you structure your day is up to you. If you're most productive working early mornings or late evenings, arrange your day accordingly — just make sure your calendar and Slack status reflect when you're actually available so teammates can plan around you.

<Accordion title="What if my personal schedule changes temporarily?">
  If your availability shifts for a week or two — childcare, travel, a personal project — just give your manager a heads-up and update your calendar. Temporary changes are generally fine as long as you stay reachable during overlap hours and keep your commitments. Longer-term changes to your schedule should be discussed with your manager directly.
</Accordion>
