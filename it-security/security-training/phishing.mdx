Phishing is the most common vector for account compromise at Autex, and most attacks work by exploiting urgency and impersonation. Attackers count on you acting before you think. Understanding the patterns these messages follow — and knowing exactly what to do when you spot one — is the most effective defence available.

## Common phishing patterns

Each of the patterns below appears regularly in real attacks. Expand each one to understand what to look for and how to respond.

<AccordionGroup>
  <Accordion title="Urgency and pressure">
    Messages that threaten immediate consequences — "Your account will be suspended in 24 hours" or "Action required: verify now" — are designed to make you act before you think. Legitimate internal systems and external services do not issue ultimatums like this over email. If a message is pushing you to act fast, slow down instead. Verify the situation through a separate channel, such as navigating directly to the service's website or calling the sender using a number you already have on record.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Mismatched sender domains">
    An email may display a trustworthy name — such as your bank or a known vendor — while the actual sending address comes from an unrelated domain like `secure-bank-alert.net` or `autex-support.helpdesk.cc`. Always check the full email address, not just the display name. In most email clients you can hover over or tap the sender name to reveal the real address. If the domain doesn't match the organisation the sender is claiming to represent, treat the message as suspicious.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Requests for credentials">
    No legitimate internal system at Autex will ask for your password via email, and no external service you use should either. If you receive a message asking you to confirm your password, re-enter your credentials, or approve a login you didn't initiate, do not comply. If a login page appears unexpectedly after clicking a link, close it without entering anything and report the message.
  </Accordion>

  <Accordion title="Unexpected attachments">
    Treat any unexpected attachment with suspicion, even if it appears to come from someone you know. Attackers frequently spoof or compromise familiar addresses. Be especially cautious with `.exe`, `.zip`, and macro-enabled Office files (`.docm`, `.xlsm`). If you weren't expecting a file and the message doesn't give a clear, plausible reason for sending it, don't open it — report it first.
  </Accordion>
</AccordionGroup>

## What to do if you receive a suspicious email

If something feels off about a message, follow these steps — in order.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Don't click links or download attachments">
    Leave the message alone. Don't click any links, download any files, or reply to the sender. Even previewing an attachment can be risky depending on your email client settings.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Report it">
    Use the **Report Phishing** button in your email client if it's available, or forward the message as an attachment to [security@autex.co.nz](mailto:security@autex.co.nz). Reporting helps the security team identify whether others are being targeted by the same campaign.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Delete it">
    Once reported, delete the message from your inbox and empty your trash. There's no need to keep it — the security team has what they need from your report.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## If you clicked a link or entered credentials

<Warning>
  If you clicked a link in a suspicious email or entered your credentials anywhere unexpected, treat it as a live incident and act immediately — do not wait to see if anything happens.
</Warning>

Speed is critical. The faster you act, the less damage an attacker can do with compromised access.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Change your password immediately">
    Go directly to the relevant service (by typing the URL yourself, not by clicking any link) and change your password right away. If you reused that password anywhere else, change it there too.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Notify the security team">
    Email [security@autex.co.nz](mailto:security@autex.co.nz) straight away. Include the URL you visited, the time it happened, and what you entered. The team needs this detail to assess the scope and contain any exposure.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Don't wait to see if anything happens">
    It may be hours or days before visible misuse occurs. Waiting reduces the team's ability to respond effectively. Report it now, even if everything currently appears normal.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## Simulated phishing tests

The IT security team runs periodic simulated phishing campaigns to help employees recognise attacks in a low-stakes environment. If you click a link in a simulated message, you'll be directed to a short training module — this is not a disciplinary action, and it isn't recorded against you. The goal is learning, not punishment.

If you notice an unusual volume of phishing messages targeting your account specifically, let the security team know at [security@autex.co.nz](mailto:security@autex.co.nz). It may indicate that your address has been added to an active targeting list.
