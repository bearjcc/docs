On-call responsibility rotates weekly among engineers on each team. When you're on call, you're the first responder for production alerts during your shift — the person who acknowledges pages, triages issues, and kicks off the incident response process if needed. This page covers how the rotation is structured, what to do before and during your shift, and when to escalate.

## Rotation schedule

The on-call schedule lives in **NightWatch**, your team's alerting and scheduling tool. A few key rules:

- Schedules are set **4 weeks in advance** so you can plan around them.
- If you need to swap a shift, arrange it directly with a teammate and update the schedule in NightWatch yourself — don't just verbally agree and forget to update the system.
- On-call assignments are visible to the whole engineering org, so keeping the schedule accurate matters.

## New engineers and shadowing

New engineers join the on-call rotation after **60 days**. Your first few weeks on rotation are shadowed: you respond to alerts and lead the triage, but a senior engineer is on secondary and available to guide you through anything unfamiliar.

<Note>
  Shadowed rotations are about building confidence, not testing you. Ask questions freely — your secondary on-call expects it and welcomes it.
</Note>

## Before your shift

A few minutes of preparation at the start of your shift prevents scrambling later.

- **Confirm your contact details** — verify your phone number is correct in the alerting tool so pages actually reach you.
- **Check #deploys** — scan for recent deploys that were flagged as fragile or risky. These are the most likely sources of alerts during your shift.
- **Review open SEV-3 issues** — check for any lingering SEV-3 issues that could escalate if left unattended.

## During your shift

### Availability

| Time | Response target |
|------|-----------------|
| Business hours | Within 5 minutes |
| Outside business hours | Within 15 minutes |

If you can't respond to a page within those windows for any reason — you're in a meeting, dealing with something urgent, or temporarily unavailable — **escalate to your secondary immediately**. Don't let a page sit unacknowledged.

### Handling each alert

<Steps>
  <Step title="Acknowledge the alert">
    Acknowledge in the alerting tool as soon as you see the page. This stops the system from re-paging you and signals to teammates that a human is on it.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Triage: real issue or noise?">
    Spend a couple of minutes determining whether this is a genuine production problem or a misfiring alert. Check dashboards and logs. If it's noise — a flapping alert or a known false positive — resolve the page and file a ticket to fix the alert. Noisy alerts erode trust in the on-call system.
  </Step>
  <Step title="If real, follow the incident response process">
    If the issue is genuine, follow the full incident response process. Classify severity, open a war room if needed, mitigate first, and communicate. See the [Incident Response overview](/engineering/incident-response) for the step-by-step guide.
  </Step>
</Steps>

### Keeping notes

Even for small issues that don't rise to a formal incident, maintain a running Slack thread in **#incidents**. This creates a record, helps the next on-call engineer understand what happened during the shift, and gives you material for a postmortem if a pattern emerges.

## Escalation path

Escalate early. Waiting until you're deeply stuck costs time and makes recovery harder.

1. **Stuck after 15 minutes** → page your secondary on-call.
2. **Still stuck after another 15 minutes** → escalate to your engineering manager.

<Tip>
  Asking for help quickly is the right call, not a last resort. The goal is resolving the incident fast — not proving you can handle it alone.
</Tip>

## After your shift

When your shift ends, hand off cleanly to the incoming on-call engineer in **#incidents**. Your handoff message should cover:

- **What happened** — a brief summary of any alerts or incidents during your shift.
- **Current status** — what's resolved, what's still open, and what's being monitored.
- **What to watch for** — anything fragile, any alerts that fired unexpectedly, or any open changes that could cause issues.

A good handoff takes five minutes to write and saves the next engineer significant confusion.

## On-call compensation

Shifts that require you to be available outside of normal business hours are compensated. See your manager or the HR portal for current rates and how to log your shifts.
