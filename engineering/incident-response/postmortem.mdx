A postmortem is required for every SEV-1 and SEV-2 incident, and is recommended for recurring SEV-3 patterns even when no single instance was severe enough to require one. The goal is learning — not blame. A good postmortem makes your systems, processes, and tooling more resilient so the same failure mode doesn't recur.

## What "blameless" means

Blameless doesn't mean consequence-free — it means the postmortem focuses on what failed in the **system**, not on who made a mistake. That includes processes, tooling, alerting, documentation, and communication — anything that could have helped an engineer make a better decision but didn't.

Engineers make reasonable decisions based on the information available to them at the time. When something still goes wrong, the postmortem's job is to identify where the system let them down: missing observability, unclear runbooks, insufficient testing, or a deployment process that allowed a risky change through.

<Note>
  If you find yourself writing a postmortem section that reads like an indictment of one person's choices, step back and ask: what information did they have? What could the system have done to prevent or catch this earlier?
</Note>

## Timeline

Move through the postmortem process promptly while the incident is still fresh in everyone's minds.

| Milestone | Deadline |
|-----------|----------|
| Create postmortem doc from template | Within 24 hours of resolution |
| Draft timeline and contributing factors | Within 48 hours |
| Hold postmortem review meeting | Within 5 business days |
| Action items assigned and tracked in issue tracker | Within 2 weeks |

## Postmortem template

Your postmortem document should follow this structure. Each section has a specific purpose — don't skip them, even when the incident feels simple in hindsight.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Incident summary">
    Write one paragraph covering what happened, when it started, and what the impact was. This is the section most people will read — make it clear and concise. Avoid technical jargon where possible; stakeholders outside engineering will read this.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Timeline">
    A chronological log of the incident in **UTC**. Include: when the issue started, when it was detected, key actions taken (deploys, rollbacks, escalations), and when it was resolved. Err on the side of including more detail — gaps in the timeline are harder to learn from than extra entries.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Root cause">
    Describe the underlying cause of the incident. If the causal chain is complex, use the **5 Whys** technique: ask "why did this happen?" repeatedly until you reach the systemic root rather than a surface-level symptom.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Contributing factors">
    List the factors that made the incident worse, harder to detect, or harder to resolve. These are distinct from root cause — they're the things that amplified the impact or slowed the response. Examples: unclear alert thresholds, missing runbook, slow deployment rollback process.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Impact">
    Quantify the blast radius: how many users were affected, for how long, and whether any data was involved. Be specific. "Some users saw errors" is less useful than "approximately 2,400 users in the EU region were unable to log in for 47 minutes."
  </Step>
  <Step title="What went well">
    Identify what helped during the response — fast detection, a well-written runbook, good team communication, a feature flag that made rollback easy. This section matters: it reinforces practices worth keeping and gives the team something to build on.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Action items">
    List concrete, specific tasks that will prevent recurrence or improve your response next time. Each action item must have an **owner**, a clear definition of "done", and a **due date**. Aim for at least one action item per significant contributing factor identified in step 4.
  </Step>
</Steps>

## The review meeting

The postmortem review meeting is a 30–45 minute session held within 5 business days of resolution. The incident owner facilitates, and everyone who was involved in the incident response should attend.

**Agenda:**

1. **Walk through the timeline** *(10 minutes)* — the incident owner narrates the sequence of events. This surfaces any gaps or misremembered details.
2. **Discuss root cause and contributing factors** *(10 minutes)* — the group discusses whether the written analysis is accurate and complete.
3. **Review and assign action items** *(15 minutes)* — confirm each action item has an owner, a due date, and a clear definition of done.

<Tip>
  The review meeting is not the place to re-hash decisions made during the incident. Keep the discussion forward-looking: what can we change so this doesn't happen again?
</Tip>

## Tracking action items

Action items from postmortems must be filed in the issue tracker immediately after the review meeting. Each item needs:

- A clear owner (a named person, not a team)
- A description of what "done" looks like
- A due date

Your engineering manager reviews open postmortem action items in the weekly team meeting. If an action item is blocked or at risk of missing its due date, surface that early — don't let it quietly slip.

<Warning>
  Action items without owners don't get done. If nobody volunteers ownership during the review meeting, the incident owner is responsible for assigning one before the meeting ends.
</Warning>
